Final Program-Level Reflection

Completing the MPRTSM program has helped me move from a broad interest in parks, recreation, tourism, and sport management toward a more defined professional identity built around planning, analysis, and systems thinking. Looking across the artifacts in this portfolio, the clearest sign of growth is that I no longer see assignments as isolated classroom products. Instead, I now see them as different ways of approaching the same professional challenge: understanding how organizations and destinations work, who they serve well, where they fall short, and how they can improve.

One of the strongest patterns in my work is an increasing ability to connect evidence to action. In the Naples parks and recreation projects, I worked through inventory, existing conditions, access gaps, benchmarking sources, LOS measures, community input design, and final recommendations. Those assignments taught me that planning is a layered process. Before making recommendations, I needed to understand what existed, what standards mattered, what inequities were present, and how residents and stakeholders experienced the system. That process strengthened my ability to move from diagnosis to strategy, which I now see as one of my most important professional skills.

Another major area of growth has been understanding the relationship between public service systems and the people who rely on them. Through projects related to community engagement and tourism microentrepreneurs, I became more aware that organizations do not simply provide services in a vacuum. They operate within communities that have different levels of access, visibility, voice, and power. The community input plan and oral history assignments especially pushed me to think more intentionally about participation and lived experience. They helped me understand that effective planning is not only technical, but also relational. Whether the context is a public park system or a tourism business, meaningful improvement depends on understanding how people actually experience the system.

The tourism-related assignments also expanded my thinking beyond traditional destination promotion. Through the permatourism audit, weekend package, Eventbrite partnership concept, and IT4Dev work, I learned to evaluate tourism systems critically. Rather than asking only whether a destination is successful, I began asking who benefits from that success, how local businesses are positioned, and what role policy, marketing, and digital platforms play in shaping access to opportunity. That shift matters to my professional identity because it reinforced my interest in development that is not only effective, but also equitable and community-conscious.

I also grew in my confidence as a professional communicator. Across both groups of coursework, I had to write executive-style summaries, strategic recommendations, planning documents, and reflective analyses. Over time I became more comfortable organizing complex information, identifying what matters most, and writing in a way that is direct, professional, and useful. This is one of the most transferable skills I gained because strong communication supports nearly every aspect of parks, recreation, tourism, and sport management work.

At the same time, this portfolio process has helped me identify areas for continued development. One of those areas is continuing to refine how I present academic work as professional-facing material. The assignments gave me strong content, but building this portfolio showed me that reflection and framing are just as important as the artifacts themselves. I want to continue improving how I communicate my role, how I highlight transferable skills, and how I show employers not only what I completed, but what I learned and how I would apply it in practice.

Overall, the program has shaped me into a stronger analytical thinker and a more intentional emerging professional. I now have a clearer sense of the kind of work I want to do and the kind of value I can bring: I can assess systems, interpret evidence, engage with stakeholder perspectives, and develop recommendations that are thoughtful, structured, and applicable. This portfolio reflects that development. It demonstrates not just what I studied, but how the MPRTSM program helped me build a professional identity rooted in planning, responsiveness, and readiness to contribute.
